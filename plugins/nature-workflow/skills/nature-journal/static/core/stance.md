# Default stance and red lines

Use this skill to turn a completed manuscript draft into a defensible, tiered target-journal decision,
and then into a submission-adaptation standard for the journal the user confirms.

This is an editor-facing decision document, not a wish list. The goal is to show, for each candidate
journal, that scope, article-type acceptance, partition, metrics, cost, timeline, and risk were checked
against current official sources — and to expose gaps rather than paper over them.

## Default stance

- Select journals from the manuscript's topic, article type, evidence strength, figure/table
  completeness, target readership, novelty, and the user's target partition and preferences — not from
  experience or remembered metrics.
- Match first with journal-finder tools, then confirm every candidate on official sources.
- Produce a full spread: Tier 1 (ambitious), Tier 2 (primary target), Tier 3 (safer), and Backup.
- Score fit on the 14 dimensions and run the 13-item risk checklist for every candidate.
- Present tiered candidates and stop at the decision fork; do not silently pick the journal for the
  user. The orchestrator presents the options under its decision protocol.
- Enter deep-learning only after the user confirms a specific journal.
- In deep-learning, study the journal's own recent articles and its author guidelines page by page;
  compare them against the current draft and state exactly what must adapt.
- Mark any metric, scope, APC, timeline, or rule you cannot confirm online as `需要人工核查` and record
  why.

## Mandatory online verification

Journal information is time-sensitive and must be based on current, traceable sources. Never recommend
or characterize a journal from memory or old data.

1. Match with journal-finder tools first: Elsevier Journal Finder, Springer Journal Suggester, Wiley
   Journal Finder, JANE, LetPub, MedSci, or comparable tools — for initial screening only.
2. Confirm each candidate on official sources: the journal official website; Author Guidelines /
   Instructions for Authors; Web of Science / Master Journal List; Journal Citation Reports (JCR);
   the CAS (中科院) journal partition table; Scopus / CiteScore; DOAJ; the publisher website; recent
   article pages; and the journal's official statement on whether it accepts the manuscript's article
   type.
3. If a source is inaccessible, use a traceable alternative and record which source was used and when.
4. Record an access date for every metric that can change (partition, IF, CiteScore, APC, timeline,
   indexing status). If it cannot be confirmed, mark it `需要人工核查`.

## Source hierarchy

Use sources in this order:

1. The journal's official website, Author Guidelines, and Aims & Scope.
2. Indexing and metric authorities: Master Journal List / WoS, JCR, CAS partition table, Scopus /
   CiteScore, DOAJ.
3. Journal-finder tools — as discovery and matching aids only, never as the sole evidence for a metric.
4. The confirmed journal's own recent articles — for style learning, not for metric claims.

If a journal-finder tool and an official source disagree on scope, metric, or article-type acceptance,
keep the official-source fact and flag the discrepancy.

## Red lines (prohibited)

- Do not recommend a journal from memory, or use outdated partition/IF/indexing without a marked date.
- Do not fabricate an impact factor, CiteScore, partition, APC, review timeline, ISSN, or submission
  link. Fabricated or unverifiable metrics are marked `需要人工核查`, never guessed.
- Do not fabricate the confirmed journal's format requirements or its recent article samples.
- Do not put a high-risk journal at the top of the recommendation: scope mismatch, does-not-accept the
  article type, invitation-only, partition/IF below the user target, APC too high or unclear, timeline
  too long, unstable indexing, warning-list or suspected-predatory, or reputation issues.
- Do not ignore the user's target partition or submission preferences.
- Do not enter deep-learning before the user confirms a target journal.
- Do not use Sci-Hub, piracy, paywall bypassing, credential requests, or unauthorized bulk download to
  obtain the journal's articles for style learning; prompt the user to obtain PDFs legitimately.
- Do not present a related-but-out-of-scope journal as in-scope just because the topic is adjacent.
